History
The National Security Agency (NSA) was originally known as Armed Forces Security Agency (AFSA), which was created on May 20, 1949. AFSA, however, failed to achieve its goal of a centralized intelligence community due to its inability to coordinate properly with civilian agencies with similar goals, such as the FBI. In light of this failure, the agency was overhauled, strengthened, and rebranded as the NSA in 1952. It has a storied history of protecting the United States from its enemies, providing it with the intelligence needed to capture and remove the threat of enemy spies within its borders while also providing the government with insight into what the USA's enemies are thinking and planning.1 However, spurred by the recent emergence of terrorism as a significant threat to national security on September 11, 2011, the NSA has steadily grown its intelligence gathering capabilities to the point where it has begun ignoring the Constitution and American law by wiretapping American citizens without first obtaining a warrant and by obtaining massive amounts of metadata about what American citizens are doing over the internet.2,3 In order to manage these expanded operations, the NSA has begun building a new facility in Utah said to be capable of storing data at the level of yottabytes (1 yottabyte = 1 trillion terabytes) of data.4
